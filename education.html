Mathematics That Equips, Not Just Informs

LMI develops curricula and teaching that give students genuine mathematical tools — the kind of depth that survives contact with hard problems in AI, data science, and the sciences.

Teaching Philosophy

Rigor as a
practical tool

The most common failure in mathematical education for AI and data science is a confusion of familiarity with understanding. Students learn to apply methods — gradient descent, matrix decomposition, neural network training — without acquiring the mathematical language to diagnose why those methods work, when they will fail, and what to do when they do.

LMI’s teaching is built on the conviction that rigorous mathematical training is not an obstacle to applied work — it is its prerequisite. A student who understands the Lax-Milgram theorem can design a PINN loss function with genuine guarantees. A student who understands spectral graph theory can ask principled questions about message-passing neural networks.

This means courses are designed around real mathematical objects — functions, operators, spaces, invariants — rather than around techniques. It means proofs are included not as formalism for its own sake, but because the proof is often where the insight lives. And it means projects use real data, because that is where mathematical thinking earns its keep.

“The purpose of abstraction is not to be vague, but to be exactly right at every level of detail.”

“Understanding a result well enough to teach it is different from understanding it well enough to use it. Both matter.”

In Authorship

Discrete Thinking: Networks, Logic, and AI with Python

A mathematically rigorous undergraduate textbook integrating discrete mathematics, graph theory, logic, and foundational AI — developed from over fifteen years of teaching experience and refined through active classroom use.

The book is distinctive in its commitment to proof-based exposition alongside Python implementations, insisting that computational fluency and mathematical understanding reinforce rather than substitute for each other. Every major concept is accompanied by both a formal treatment and a working implementation applied to real datasets.

Topics are sequenced to build progressively toward machine learning applications, culminating in graph neural networks, logical inference systems, and a treatment of discrete optimization grounded in combinatorial theory.

Discrete Thinking Series — Course I

Graph Theory and Network Modeling with Python

In Development

A rigorous yet accessible course on graph theory and its applications to real-world network modeling, developed as the first installment of the Discrete Thinking series. The course builds from first principles — graphs as mathematical objects, paths and connectivity, trees, planarity — and advances to applied topics including centrality measures, community detection, and spectral graph theory.

Throughout, Python and NetworkX are used not as a substitute for mathematical understanding but as a tool for making abstract structures visible and testable. Students work with real datasets: transportation networks, social graphs, and scientific citation networks. By the end of the course, students can both prove the theorems and run the code.

Consulting Services

Mathematical Rigor for Research and Industry

LMI offers consulting to research teams, academic departments, and technology organizations that require mathematical expertise in the design, analysis, or validation of machine learning pipelines — particularly those involving physics-based or scientifically constrained models.

This is not general ML consulting. The value LMI provides is specifically the kind of rigorous mathematical analysis that connects the behavior of a model to provable properties of the underlying mathematical objects — error bounds, convergence guarantees, regularity conditions, and identifiability constraints.
