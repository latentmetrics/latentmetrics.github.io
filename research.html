Research Program

Mathematics and Engineering of Physics-Informed Neural Networks

LMI’s research develops the mathematical foundations of Physics-Informed Neural Networks — examining error structure, regularity conditions, and convergence behavior to establish when and how reliably these models encode physical law.

Research Focus

What are the latent metrics of physical systems?

The central question driving LMI’s research is precise: under what mathematical conditions do Physics-Informed Neural Networks reliably encode physical law, and how fast do they converge? PINNs embed PDE residuals directly into the training loss, but having no built-in obligation to satisfy conservation laws, boundary conditions, or the regularity constraints of the underlying equation, the conditions under which they actually succeed remain incompletely understood.

LMI approaches this problem through the lens of classical analysis. The tools are functional analysis and Sobolev theory for regularity, regularization theory for understanding how Leray-type mollification affects solution structure, numerical analysis for error bounds and convergence guarantees, and operator learning for generalizing across entire families of parametric PDEs.

The goal is not simply to apply these methods, but to establish provable, quantitative guarantees — theorems and bounds that tell us precisely when, and how well, a physics-informed model can be trusted.

Current Projects

Active Research Lines

Project 01

Error Analysis and Numerical Validation of PINNs for the Leray-Burgers Equation

The Leray-Burgers equation applies Leray regularization to the classical Burgers equation, replacing the nonlinear advection term with a mollified velocity. This smoothing makes the equation analytically tractable but introduces a regularization parameter whose effect on PINN approximation quality is poorly understood.

This project develops a comprehensive error analysis framework for PINN approximations of the 1D Leray-Burgers equation, deriving theoretical bounds on the residual loss, the approximation error in Sobolev norms, and the regularity of the learned solution. Three test cases with distinct solution regimes (smooth, boundary-layer, multi-scale) provide the basis for structured numerical experiments validating the theoretical predictions.

Project 02

Operator Learning and Latent Space Methods for Parametric PDEs

Standard neural network PDE solvers learn a solution for a fixed set of parameters. Operator learning reframes the task: instead of approximating a function, the network approximates the solution operator that maps input functions (initial conditions, forcing terms, coefficients) to output functions. This generalization is mathematically non-trivial and requires careful treatment of function-space topology.

This line of work investigates the approximation theory underpinning operator learning — in particular, how properties of the latent representation space (continuity, compactness, metric structure) govern the generalization capacity of learned operators across PDE families relevant to fluid dynamics and heat transfer.

Project 03

Graph-Theoretic Models of Complex Physical and Informational Networks

Many physical and social systems — fluid transport networks, neural architectures, citation graphs, communication infrastructure — share structural properties best captured by graph theory. This project develops and applies graph-theoretic models to real-world network datasets, with emphasis on identifying invariants (spectral properties, centrality distributions, community structure) that predict system behavior under perturbation.

Applications include analysis of lunar surface topology using NASA LOLA elevation data, Premier League passing network analysis, and centrality-based robustness measures for infrastructure graphs. The project also feeds directly into the undergraduate curriculum in discrete mathematics and AI.

Research Interests

Broader Mathematical Landscape

Neural Networks & Operators

Physics-Informed Neural Networks, neural operators, approximation theory in function spaces, and convergence guarantees for PDE-constrained learning.

Nonlinear PDEs & Dynamics

Regularized fluid equations, Leray-type mollification, well-posedness theory, and the behavior of solutions under perturbation of the governing parameters.

Scientific Machine Learning

Hybrid SciML pipelines, physically consistent modeling, error structure in learned solutions, and the relationship between training loss and solution accuracy.

Data Analytics

Network analysis, real-world dataset modeling, and quantitative methods bridging mathematical theory and applied data-driven investigation.

AI for Mathematics & Education

Curriculum design at the boundary of mathematics and AI, and the use of computational tools to make rigorous mathematical thinking more accessible.
